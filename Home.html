The Global Challenge to Achieving Zero Hunger

Help us provide food and hope to those in need.

Zero Hunger is the second goal of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), aiming to end hunger, achieve food security, and improve nutrition by 2030.

It seeks to ensure that everyone, especially vulnerable populations, has access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food throughout the year. This goal also promotes sustainable agriculture, encouraging practices that increase food production while protecting the environment.

Achieving Zero Hunger requires reducing food waste, supporting small-scale farmers, and improving food distribution systems. By working toward this goal, the world can eliminate malnutrition and create a future where no one suffers from hunger.

When Did It Start?

The Zero Hunger initiative is part of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), specifically Goal 2: Zero Hunger, which was established in September 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development.

Current Challenges

Hover to see details

As of March 2025, progress
toward achieving Zero Hunger, as outlined in Sustainable Development Goal 2 (SDG 2), has encountered significant challenges. In Bangladesh, the United Nations plans to reduce food rations for Rohingya refugees from $12.50 to $6 per month starting April 1, due to funding shortages, raising concerns about increased hunger in refugee camps.

Zero Hunger aims to end hunger
and ensure nutritious food for all by 2030. It focuses on reducing malnutrition, supporting small farmers, and promoting sustainable farming. Protecting food resources and improving rural development are key goals. Stronger policies and funding are needed to succeed.
